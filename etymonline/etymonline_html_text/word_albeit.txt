<blockquote><span class="foreign">Artiste</span>: an admirable word (albeit somewhat Frenchified) of late applied, with nice discrimination, to every species of exhibitor, from a rope-dancer down to a mere painter or sculptor. On looking into little Entick (my great authority in these matters), I find we have already the word <span class="foreign">artist</span>; but with stupid English perversity, we have hitherto used that in a much more restricted sense than its newly-imported rival, which it is becoming the excellent fashion to adopt. [&quot;Paul Pry&#x27;s Journal of a Residence at Little-Pedlington,&quot; Philadelphia, 1836]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/artiste#etymonline_v_17057"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of comedy" href="/word/comedy#etymonline_v_15861">comedy (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;narrative with a happy ending; any composition intended for amusement,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">comedie</span> (14c.), &quot;a poem&quot; (not in the theatrical sense) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">comoedia</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">komoidia</span> &quot;a comedy, amusing spectacle,&quot; probably from <span class="foreign">komodios</span> &quot;actor or singer in the revels,&quot; from <span class="foreign">komos</span> &quot;revel, carousal, merry-making, festival&quot; + <span class="foreign">aoidos</span> &quot;singer, poet,&quot; from <span class="foreign">aeidein</span> &quot;to sing,&quot; related to <span class="foreign">oide</span> (see <span class="crossreference">ode</span>).</p>
<blockquote>The passage on the nature of comedy in the <span class="foreign">Poetic</span> of Aristotle is unfortunately lost, but if we can trust stray hints on the subject, his definition of comedy (which applied mainly to Menander) ran parallel to that of tragedy, and described the art as a purification of certain affections of our nature, not by terror and pity, but by laughter and ridicule. [Rev. J.P. Mahaffy, &quot;A History of Classical Greek Literature,&quot; London, 1895]</blockquote>
<p>The origin of Greek <span class="foreign">komos</span> is uncertain; perhaps it is from a PIE <span class="foreign">*komso-</span> &quot;praise,&quot; and cognate with Sanskrit <span class="foreign">samsa</span> &quot;praise, judgment.&quot; Beekes suggests Pre-Greek. The old derivation from <span class="foreign">kome</span> &quot;village&quot; is not now regarded.</p>
<p>The classical sense of the word was &quot;amusing play or performance with a happy ending,&quot; which is similar to the modern one, but in the Middle Ages the word meant poems and stories generally (albeit ones with happy endings), such as Dante&#x27;s <span class="foreign">&quot;Commedia.&quot;</span> The revival of learning 16c. recovered the ancient comedies and shifted the sense of the word to &quot;branch of drama addressing primarily the humorous and ridiculous&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">tragedy</span>). In 18c. this was somewhat restricted to &quot;humorous, but not grossly comical, drama&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">farce</span>).</p>

<blockquote><span class="foreign">Comedy</span> aims at entertaining by the fidelity with which it presents life as we know it, <span class="foreign">farce</span> at raising laughter by the outrageous absurdity of the situation or characters exhibited, &amp; <span class="foreign">burlesque</span> at tickling the fancy of the audience by caricaturing plays or actors with whose style it is familiar. [Fowler]</blockquote>

<p>Meaning &quot;comic play or drama&quot; is from 1550s (the first modern comedy in English was said to be Nicholas Udall&#x27;s &quot;Roister Doister&quot;). Extended sense &quot;humorous or comic incident or events in life&quot; is from 1560s. Generalized sense of &quot;quality of being amusing&quot; dates from 1877. </p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
